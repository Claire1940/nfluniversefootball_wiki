The most important setting to modify immediately is **Sprint Zoom**. When Sprint Zoom is active, sprinting widens your field of view (FOV). If you are scrambling under pressure and suddenly stop to throw, the camera snaps back instantly. This sudden focal shift pulls your reticle down, causing your pass to fly dangerously low and straight into the hands of a waiting defensive back. Disabling this feature ensures your visual field remains static, guaranteeing predictable throw arcs.

Additionally, setting your personal **Time of Day** to **Night** in your settings menu is a pro-level trick. Bright daylight on fields, especially in the Park, can produce severe glare that obscures deep routes and makes tracking the ball difficult. Playing under night lighting provides a high-contrast, clean visual environment.

<Callout type="tip" title="Sensitivity Calibration">
Start with a **QB Aim Sensitivity** of 30. If you find yourself over-leading your receivers and throwing out of bounds, lower it by 5. If your reticle feels too sluggish to track fast-burning routes, increase it slightly. Controller players should lean toward a higher sensitivity (around 40 to 45) to offset joystick physical limitations.
</Callout>

<Section icon={Target} title="Throwing Types and Power Levels">

In NFL Universe Football, managing your throwing power and understanding when to use specific pass types is what separates average quarterbacks from elite playmakers. While throwing at 100 power is the standard for most plays, subtle adjustments allow you to fit the ball into tight windows.

| Power Level | Recommended Use Case | Execution Technique |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **100 Power** | Bullet passes, crossing routes, deep endzone targets | Step forward into the throw to keep the ball flat and fast. |
| **95 Power** | Sweet spot lob passes, corner fits, deep fades | Release with a high camera angle to lob over DB heads. |
| **90 Power** | Medium-range routes, crossing slants, seams | Zoom out fully to read the middle of the field before release. |
| **85 Power** | Short checkdowns, comeback routes, quick flats | Look slightly down and release quickly to avoid blitz pressure. |

There are two primary passing styles in the game: bullet passes and lob passes. Bullet passes are executed by stepping forward in your movement direction while releasing the ball. This flattens the trajectory and gets the ball to the receiver as fast as possible, which is essential for hitting tight slants or beating close coverage near the numbers.

Lob passes, on the other hand, require you to step back (creating a fadeaway motion) or jump while aiming your camera upwards. This creates a high, sweeping arc that sails over the heads of defensive backs. Use lob passes for corner routes and deep streaks where your wide receiver has a step on the defender.

<Callout type="warning" title="Avoid Low Power Passes">
Never throw passes below 85 power unless executing a highly specific short checkdown. Low-power throws have excessive air time and lack velocity, turning standard routes into easy interceptions for alert defenders.
</Callout>

Use these sequential steps to develop your on-field execution:

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Throw with Anticipation">
    Do not throw the ball directly at your receiver's current position. Instead, throw the ball to where they are going to be. For deep streaks, aim approximately five yards ahead of their run. For slant routes, time your release so the ball meets them right as they cut across the numbers.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Sync Your Movement and Release">
    To maximize your throw's anticipation and velocity, run in the direction of your target immediately before releasing the pass. This mechanical synergy adds momentum to the ball, making it easier to lead your receiver perfectly.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Master Crossbody Throws">
    Throwing crossbody (aiming opposite to your movement direction) is highly effective but mechanically demanding. Because crossbody throws take longer to travel, you must release the ball earlier and aim slightly higher than you would on a standard straight pass.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="Call Plays and Use Pad Boosts">
    Always call plays to coordinate with your team. When your wide receiver steps onto a play-call pad, they receive a temporary speed boost. Time your deep throws to release exactly as they hit this pad to burn the defensive coverage deep.
  </Step>
</Steps>
